The important thing to keep in mind when selecting organizations for Role Mining is that each chosen organization will have a workshop with a business manager. Therefore, it is important to target a reasonable number of organizations so as not to have too many workshops, as each workshop must be planned and prepared and business managers must be solicited and available.

The selection of organizations to be included in the Role Mining analysis must meet three criteria:

  • The first criterion is organizational coverage. This involves specifying which organizations are to be included in the analysis. This choice is a direct result of a project decision. For example, if the objective is to create roles related to finance, the selection should focus on the Finance Department and any others that would be related to finance.
  • The second criterion is a notion of organizational depth. The size of the organization is an important decision element. The idea is to stay on levels of the organization that allow the following two points to be reconciled:
    • Organizations must contain a minimum number of people. For example, a department with only two people is too small. This is because, during the workshop, the pivot table will only show the rights of two people. Roles based on too few people are not generic and create a higher risk of having too many roles at the end of the project. This model is difficult to maintain.
    • Organizations should not contain too many people. If the selected organization is too high up in the hierarchy and contains too many people, the business manager will not be able to accurately describe the activity of all of them.
      On the other hand, the closer the organization is to top management, the more difficult it will be for managers to be available for workshops.
    Organizations with a staff of between 10 and 100 people should be given preference in the selection. It is ideal to choose organizations with a few dozen people because they present the best compromise on the following points:
    • The number of people is sufficient to create generic roles.
    • The manager knows each employee and his function, which helps to create quality roles.
  • The third criterion is related to the maintenance of the created roles. When the role model is implemented, it will have to be periodically reviewed in order to align the roles with the evolution of the business in the company. Therefore, an owner who is responsible for maintaining the roles will need to be designated. There are several possible strategies, which include:
    • Each business manager who participates in the workshops to create the roles is also responsible for their maintenance.
    • A dedicated department (central or local, for example) is responsible for role maintenance.
    At the time of selection, it is important to address who will be entrusted with the role maintenance responsibility.
